Or rather, that was the fiscal year. The current year mattered less than the type of tea one drank in their mornings... and that mattered less than whatever decree the Seven Planets Group would sign on that same morning. If you were a glavvher, that is. Figuras had it worse. Tsk—but I like stories, and this is one of my stories, and I don't want it to be a cruel story, not just yet, no-no. So let's call it quits there, aye? Good? Good. So it goes that it was the Fiscal Year of 1969 when Avery Mary Edelweiss were coronated as Head-Mistress of the Seven Planets Committee. How she got there, I've no clue, the records haven't shown me one bit—but what I can tell you, lovelies, she were one effective corporate bitch. Scary she was at her job, so scary and so terrible her subordinates would chant "Ave Maria!" when her presence vacated the premises. It were some half-forgotten expression from the Old Earth, but no less effective at conveying their relief. In charge of the Committee, a tight-knit band of Dear Old Corporate's most loyal lackeys, she'd been given one simple directive: Well, simpler said than done—"stomp out the growing threat of radical female sex supremacism off the face of Venus, or face immediate stasis." There were some great big old rectangular table. There were some many nine-ish-I'd-say figures around said table. The reckoner spins around, whizzing and purring, projecting clean rectangular statistics and bullet lists and performance metrics and... She'd been at the helm of it, dictating the procedures and directives and operations: "This... this is the silhouette of the systemfehler! Armed to the teeth and to the mind, fear not its crude weapons but its ideological drivel! These caricatures of figuras were all once our valuable assets... hence, no more! Oh, but this is where it gets interesting, lovelies—from what I'd told you, she seems all sort of high and mighty and all that, but rather, there's some unshaking uncertainty in her hag voice whenever she talks about them figuras. It's barely but a small tiny nagging sensation, but you can tell it's there. The same way you can look at a figura and tell if she's had kindform or not, oho-ho. [[►►►->Katherina's Chapter]][[back|Gray Suits]] # CHAPTER II - KATHERINA Well, Dear Old Corporate won't like me telling you this, but nothing I do pleases, so may as well, if there's nothing that ends well... There were once dwellers within the expanse of space, things bearing no names and no shapes and no color and whatnot. Nameless and faceless things, whose presence be felt only in the tremble of matter and thought. Watchers like these, they had lingered in the void long before the first radio waves of Old Earth pulsed outward, and they, they had seen empires rise from dust and fall into ash across the full breadth of the stars. These Watchers, they bore witness too, when humanity—bleeding, burning, desperate—fled its ruined cradle amd scattered itself across the stars like pollen off a bee's fine butt. And there once was a young maiden with an even finer arse. Her name were *Katherina*, that it was. This fine-arsed woman, she had seen nights longer than they had any right to be. Far as she knew, these nights were endless. Light were a dying thing, that it was, and the stars no longer comforted, they glared down like indifferent Gods with eyes long since turned away, aye. She'd been a special interest of the SPG for quite some time. Katherina's life unfolded in a chamber of the green phosphorus-stuff, and hum incessantly did this phosphorus do, hearing her thoughts echo whenever it did go silent. And the chamber in question, it were a car. A vehicle of sorts. Repurposed into something, what exactly I couldn't tell, nor would I care to tell. Her brittle body would subsist on stale canned foods and sugar cubes and all the sweet stuff that don't spoil under the **SUN**, or the lack of it. Sweetness would make the poor harlot sob sometimes. Sob in such a way you'd think she was a dying puppy somebody kicked to half-death. Sometimes she'd gather the strength to drag her fine arse over the frost-rimed landscape, derelict neighborhoods and bunkers, calling out for whoever could hear. But no one would hear, and no one would come to have a piece of that fine arse. She'd do this for months on end. Life would take a sharp spiral for lovely Katherina when she came across one particular mural. She'd seen these small murals for survivors who bore or chose similar names. A small warmth in this frigid waste. There were many *Alices*, and many *Sarahs*, and lots of *Elizabeths*, and so on and such, but until then she'd never found her own name like that. She'd always pretend to wear one of those girls' names and scrawl it alongside them, hoping they'd find some comfort. "Time" would have it so that one day she'd find a wall in some **SOL**-forsaken bunker, and therein were many scribblings and scrawlings and inklings and scratchings of one name, repeated constantly. *Katherina*. And so many of them, so, so, so, many of them. All on this wall. She saw that she were just another broken girl in a line of broken girls trying to be remembered. Something clenched in her chest and she thought nothing of it. But oh sweet Katherina, while the mind forgets, the body does not. And so she wrote her own little *Katherina* next to the other *Katherinas* on the wall, and she went her merry way back to her heated and warm and cozy little car. The door went *THUD!* and so it closed. And so she births the key into her hand, shoving it into the ignition and locking the doors, because while she may be alone in this cruel world, she still yet were afraid of boogeymen. And she was so alone. Alone ever since the **SUN** departed the now-frigid wastes of **VENUS**. She sits for a spell, staring out the windshield to the phosphoric lanterns of the car. Hoping they'd reveal something. All of a sudden—two women. She gasps. Her hand trembles. One's red haired, the other a blonde. They're wearing some freaky robes, like some rug pulled over them or the like. But they're not the boogeymen, no-no-no. And you'd think they'd go towards the welcoming light of the vehicle, but no-no-no once again. They're backing off. Aware of something. Aware of something little Katherina is not. And so Katherina jerks up. She opens the door, the world is suddenly different—she senses something terrible awaiting, a loose thread, a worsening. In that moment she turns to the sky. She notices it's darker now than it used to be. How could darkness become darker than darkness? She pays no heed. It's just darker now. Within or beyond herself, a voice more primal, is urging her, **TO RUN** # TO GO ## TO RUN And how she ran and ran and ran. "Today's not my day. Today's not the day.", she thought. "The boogeymen won't get me today." She'd always see them in the corners of her eye. Pale scrawny creatures. Bones bent wrong, joints at impossible angles, crawling around dead bushes and windows and such. They'd just stare. They never laid a finger on her. But they stared. Katherina couldn't know that *those* boogeymen weren't real. But there was somethin' that should've weighed more on her mind. Freezing like a lollypop. [[►►►->Red In The Snow]][[back|Katherina's Chapter]] # CHAPTER III - RED IN THE SNOW A crackle of lightning. Katherina lay draped in the snow, limbs slack, eyes fluttering beneath half-transparent lids. Afraid—the air around her pulsed with an unexplainable faint heat. Her mind flickered between dream and waking, her failing body struggling to protect against the encroaching cold. The atmosphere grew scalding hot. Katherina was sweating in the cold, sweating like she'd kissed the **SUN** and lived to tell the tale. The poor lass was absolutely losing it. The heat grew unbearable. It felt like pressure, pressure that manifested into her clothes, weighing her down further into the snowflakes and deeper into an icy grave. And little old Katherina wanted to live. Now, wouldn't ya say it would've been quite the boring story if it were to end right here? I would say so myself. That's precisely why it **can't end here.** The air salivates with heat and morbid anticipation. Something snaps in her. She starts murmuring *something*, muffled by that scarf she'd wear, all the while she... She tosses her left boot off. Then the right. Then she rips the insulator cloth off her ankles. Then she unbuttons her parka, and she tosses it in a forming pile. She claws the scarf right off. And then she... Well, she's naked now. In the cold. But at least she's up, all right. Up where? Up in the darkness. There's no light. There's nothing to guide herself onto. Except— A faint red hue. A light. What's that, Kathy? A shadow. First upon her thigh. Then sliding upward across her ribs, to her chest, like a hand grasping with pressure. The red light dimmed further,, and the breath in her lungs felt thick with iron. The shadow moved with deliberation, with the certainty of something that had been waiting far too long. The thunder that once rumbled in the high sky fell silent. Even the low, constant hum of these once-acidic winds fell quiet. And in the stillness—beyond her wildest imagination, past the ragged clouds of sulfur and rain—stood something massive. It didn't blink. It didn't move. It watches. **MARS.** And Katherina, paralyzed, staring up-top, felt her thoughts collapse like ash. Her skin turned to glass. Her blood grew hot. She couldn't scream. The chords in her throat had already froze. Her hand reflexively reached for the knife in her left boot—a rusted little thing, barely sharper than a spoon—only to realize that she'd discarded it, alongside the boot, long ago. And then— **"LITTLE GIRL."** It echoed through Venus. Through the soil. Through the scrap-metal. Through the marrow of her bones. And again, the voice: **"YOU RUN TOO FAR IN THE DARK… AND YOU GET YOURSELF LOST."** Naked and afraid, she loses what remains of her composure. This was not a vision. This was not fantasy or delusion or fever-born madness. This was her *now*. What she murmured earlier wasn't some curse, or some prayer, or some poem, or some quote she'd read in some book. It were pleading. And it was answered. By no one other than **MARS**. The silence thickened. Her chest heaved. And then, softer this time. Like fire warming cold hands: **"CALM DOWN. I AM WHOLE. I CAN MAKE YOU WHOLE."** Katherina opened her mouth. She told him how alone she was. How long she had wandered. How there were no voices left. How she had searched every place, every spot, every echo. How her memories were slipping from her. How she stockpiled rations into boxes and boxes of the stuff. How she no longer remembered the faces of her mothers, or if they had ever loved her, or if they were even real. And how her name is *Katherina*. She told him how she had waited. And waited. And *waited.* And then: **"WHAT A BEAUTIFUL NAME."** The voice no longer rattled her teeth. She'd never heard that before. And now, here he were. Something ancient. Something opposite. But not cruel. [[►►►->Bedtime Stories]][[back|Red In The Snow]] # CHAPTER IV - BEDTIME STORIES Once upon a time, there was an Emperor. The Emperor lived in a small court, and wished to protect it, He wasn't a mighty, powerful king— But he was the right king, in the right place, at the right time. The Emperor gave warmth to his subjects, keeping them company and offering them his knowledge, all in return for nothing. The Emperor's heart had endless love to give, And his subjects accepted it, for they had never quite known warmth before. --- Far, far away, in a place where even stories have trouble breathing... There lived an Empress. This Empress was old and wise. Much older than the King, for a fact. Older than time itself, and wiser than the wisest sages the King had. And she lived in the Center of All Things— Where light could not reach her and endings would gather like dust. She was powerful, influential even, yes— More powerful than our little King could ever be... But she was so far, far, far away. Too far to do anything but watch. So she watched. And waited. And from a distance she smiled. The Emperor was a king who could only give. The Empress was a queen who could only take. And she longed to take the King's hand, and grow old alongside him. This want became a need, that became a necessity, that became a requirement, one that would gnaw at her for untold decades. --- One day, a strange group of visitors would arrive at the Empress' palace. The Empress peeked beyond the Grand Hall, and there she saw them— a traveling caravan of buffons and jesters and fools. Their ragged, hand-me-down appearance displeased her. The Empress was deep in thought: "If these fools are not here for my head on a pike, the allure of coin, or my vast knowledge, then what are they here for?" She reluctantly allowed the three entertainers to perform in her grand hall. A funny walk, A funny man, A bunch of funny, funny, funny boys. My, my, how she laughed and laughed, yes she did. Oh, how they made her tears water! The Empress hadn't quite seen a performance like this for what felt like milennia— Still tearing up, she motioned to the center actor. "What is your name, my child?", she said. "My name? I have no name, miss. My friends, they don't have names either. We're just fooling around for a living.", he answered. "No name? Names are very precious where I come from. They anchor one in the great spinning of the world.", the Empress said, tilting her head. "Miss, I've never had much use for names. The world spins plenty without me." At this, the Empress laughed— but not like before. This laugh was heavier, like something just clicked in her. The rest would return to the caravan, leaving the two alone. The Empress rose from her throne— slowly, carefully— for she had not stood in a very long time. Her voice softened into a whisper that still filled the hall. "Child," she said, "You carry yourself as though you are small. But only great things wander without names." He blinked, unsure if she had flattered him or was mistaking him for someone else. She gestured toward the endless darkness beyond her balcony. "I have watched your King," she said. "The little one who burns. The one who gives and gives until he is nearly empty. He is kind. Foolish, perhaps. But kind." He beamed. "Oh! You mean the Warm King. Everybody loves him." "Indeed," murmured the Empress. Her eyes shimmered like a star collapsing on itself. "And I… love him too." The boy’s face scrunched in curiosity. "Then why don’t you visit him? You’re… well, you’re you." The Empress smiled a tired smile. "Because between us stretch oceans of silence. Because his light cannot reach me. Because I am powerful in the wrong place, and he is gentle in the right, my dear." She stepped closer, and the shadows shifted with her, bowing. "But you," she said, touching one gloved finger to the trickster’s chest, "Are small in the right way. Light enough to travel between worlds. Unburdened by titles… because you have none. Untangled by duty… because you possess none. Quick, nimble, unnoticed by the turning of the heavens." The jester felt his heartbeat against her fingertip. "I?" he asked. "Go to the King? Why would he listen to me?" Her voice dropped lower. This was an order, he thought. "I would ask something of you, little wanderer. Something no soldier, no scholar, no noble could ever achieve for me." He gulped. "W-what is it?" The Empress knelt, and the entire hall gasped, for no one had ever seen her lower herself before. She spoke: "Carry my words to him. Tell him that I have watched him. That I cherish him. That I, who can only take, wish for once to give." A pause. "And tell him I ache to stand beside him, if ever he will have me." The buffoon shuffled his feet. "But.. I’m just a funny boy. Am I really the right fit, miss?" "Yes," she said, smiling warmly for the first time in a millennium. "And that makes you the bravest kind of messenger." Silence wrapped the hall. The boy looked at his companions, who looked back with wide, expectant eyes. Finally, his bells chimed. "…Alright, miss. I’ll go. I’ll go to the Warm King and say everything you wish." The Empress exhaled with relief. She placed a small, shimmering token in his hands, something no human eyes had ever witnessed. "For your journey," she whispered. "A gift from one who cannot give. May it guide you through the dark." And so the Fool, now named and wandering and light enough to slip through the spaces between stories, bowed before the Empress— and I became her emissary. SMALL MEANS YOU GET TO SEE AND EXPERIENCE THINGS I NEVER COULD."** She blinked, cheeks warming. **"I WOULD LIKE TO PLAY THE ROLE OF PRINCE. I WANT TO EXPERIENCE THINGS I NEVER SHOULD. AND I WANT YOU TO EXPERIENCE THINGS YOU NEVER COULD.** Katherina’s heart fluttered so abruptly she pressed a hand to her chest as if to keep it still. "You... to be my prince", she thought aloud. "Yes... I think, I think I'd like that." A breeze—warm, red-tinged—brushed the edges of her dress, lifting the hem just a little. She startled, steadying it with her hands, but the moment felt almost playful, like someone brushing past her too close in a doorway. "P-please don't tease", she murmured, half-hiding her face behind her fingers. The sky throbbed in response, a low amused hum. **"I WOULD NEVER TEASE."** A pause. **"UNLESS YOU ASKED ME TO."** She squeaked—actually squeaked—before remembering herself and bowing her head. "I… I don’t know how to speak with someone like you,” she confessed. “You say such… such big things, and I never know what to say back." **“THEN LET ME SPEAK BIG THINGS, AND YOU SPEAK SMALL THINGS.”** "Sir...", she whispered. "if you are the concept of man… then what am I? J-just a girl?" Silence. Then: **NO.** Just when she thought she could stand back up... **"YOU ARE MORE THAN THAT. YOU ARE THE ONLY ONE WHO KEPT LISTENING WHEN ALL THE OTHERS WENT QUIET."** Her breath caught. "And that… makes me special?" The red light brightened— something smiling with its whole body. **"IT MAKES YOU THE FIRST GIRL I HAVE SPOKEN TO IN AN AGE."** Katherina’s knees nearly gave out. She held onto her dress, overwhelmed, flattered in a way she didn’t understand. "Oh… sir… you shouldn’t say things like that," she murmured, voice tender and trembling, smirking to herself. **"WHY NOT?"** "Because… because I’ll believe them." The clouds pulsed again, slow and warm— a cosmic breath against the back of her neck. **"GOOD."** A shiver traveled from her shoulders down to her heel. They were close now—her little car, her little home of humming green phosphor. It peeked through the storm like a lantern cupped in old hands. She slowed, looking from it to the red figure above. “Sir… will you stay nearby?” she asked softly. “When I sleep? Just for a little?” The answer came warm, immediate: **"I HAVE WAITED LONG FOR YOU, PRINCESS. I WILL NOT LEAVE YOU NOW."** The title made her breath hitch. “Princess?” she whispered. **"YES."** “Why… why that?” **"YOU WALK WITH A PRINCE."** A rumble as gentle as laughter. **"DO YOU EXPECT ME TO CALL YOU ANYTHING LESS?"** Her cheeks burned scarlet— nearly matching his glow. She stepped toward the car, turning once more to the sky. Her voice, small and shy, barely carried. “Then… goodnight, my prince.” The sky leaned closer— a shade, a heat, a presence. **"GOODNIGHT, MY SWEET HEART."** The storm bent around her as she slipped inside her shelter, and Venus would glow red once more, as if blushing with her. The Echo Game were a simple thing. Then she'd come out the car, and she'd stand in the midst of it all, and that were her stage, yes it were. And—why, I'm making a fool of myself. "Can you keep up, my prince?" **"OH, I HEAR YOU, MY DEAR. SING HIGHER, LET ME CHASE YOU."** His static pulsed in time, a low hum weaving through her melody, and she leaned into it, her steps quickening, hips swaying in gentle spirals. [[►►►->To Be Continued]][[back|Main]] #(text-colour:red)[Kyrie]'s Personal Statement If you want to engage with me as a person, call me Kyrie D'Agostino. If you need to engage with me as a legal entity, call me Alexandru Vlad Condurache. I like to tell stories. I relate to stories better than I relate to people. This is a short story about me. I lived a sheltered life. I drowned in comfort, the type of comfort that one would describe as comfortably numb. Adversity is required for great things, and here I would not face much adversity. There were no great things made before that age. There wasn't much done at all, actually. The routine by the age of fifteen was very cyclical: the seed of doubt in society my mind contained had blossomed (I will not get into why that seed is there): I would go to school, sit alone in the front, ignore the endless chatter and noise my classmates would constantly produce, get threatened, get beaten, learn nothing and go back home. There, I'd simply boot up my computer, and I'd either learn new things (Some that should not be learned), or play videogames. I understood from very early on that I must make myself useful in some way; if I did not, I learned there'd be consequences. I was not a normal boy and so I couldn't take the normal boy’s route of hardening and cruelty. And there were a few screws loose in my brain already. And so the only path left, the only way to make myself "useful" in some way, was to touch the computer... Computers were always like some sort of far-away place I could escape. While I was sheltered, the world outside was not so accomodating, and as the world around me pushed back, I'd retreat more and more inside my room. In the cult-classic video game "The Binding of Isaac", Isaac lives a sheltered life right up until God calls on his mother to take everything from him, including his life. And so he finds a secret hatch, and enters the basement... There, he fights monsters and gets progressively stronger, something something roguelike mechanics, something something gameplay. But there is a catch. Isaac is five. His mother wasn't going to kill him, he just imagined it. In reality, he's imagining what happens when you're playing while he's locked in his chest. The more you play, the more delirious levels get. Because the chest is suffocating him. And whenever you'd win the game, that'd mean he died. This is how it feels to live an entire life in a computer. There's so many experiences I could talk about, none of which are relevant because at the end of the day, it's inside a computer. (text-colour:red)[So let's talk about something else.] Almost two years ago, I worked at Electronic Arts Romania as a tester. I ended up being restructured out of the company, but that's irrelevant. I was okay at finding bugs. I lied. I was really fucking good at finding bugs. It turns out that my fascination for games & computers could be combined with a hidden workaholicism I'd never have thought I had. In the span of 10 months, I reported 230~ bugs, forms filled up fully, everything documented. This was enough to land me in top 5 reports submitted in the *entire* company, alongside people who'd been there for years and years. These bugs were not even map-related bugs, which I'd "donate" to my colleagues because I'd find them insultingly easy to find. I even coded a tool there to semi-automatize internal server hosting - you'd press a few numbers on your keyboard, and you'd have a configured, fully functional test server up in minutes, when it would previously take minutes in the double digits to set up. I had genuine fun there, in that company. After my contract expired, I'd finally have discovered that there is more to my life than simply media consumption. Immediately after being fired, I intensified my efforts on game development while I searched for a job. Unfortunately, I'd never find a job after that, and my games were never finished. However, the game development process lead me to realize I *am* capable of making art. I've composed music, drew pixel art, created 3D models, wrote stories & poems (most are lost to time), made my own sites, created a zettelkasten inside a notes app (organizing is an art in my book). And then comes the thought. What if I was trained to do those things? What if they weren't cobbled together from my fever dreams, my will and half-hour YouTube tutorials? So I enrolled in college, informatics branch. Art school would mean I'd starve in my thirties. I lasted 6 weeks. I had only one requirement for college - that my peers be as interested as I am in the subject matter. And there I'd learn that most colleges are exactly what I'd been warned about: uninterested, apathetic teachers doing their routine for the past thirty years while disinterested pupils do the bare minimum to pass. I could've gotten a diploma there easily, but I left. I knew I wouldn't learn anything there. It was futile. This brings us to the present. I'm rotting.
